An Overview of Financial Statements and Analysis Techniques

In this article, we will explore key financial concepts such as assets, liabilities, net assets, cash flow forecasts, and financial statement analysis. These concepts are fundamental in understanding a company's financial health and making informed decisions.

Understanding Assets

Assets are critical resources that a company owns, and they are categorized into non-current and current assets.

Non-Current Assets

Non-current assets are those that a company plans to hold for more than a year. These assets typically include:

  • Property (such as land or factories)
  • Equipment (including machinery and technical tools)

The value of non-current assets is typically larger and often depreciated over time.

Current Assets

Current assets are expected to be converted into cash or used within a year. These assets typically include:

  • Cash
  • Accounts receivable (money owed by customers)
  • Inventory (stock of goods)

The value of current assets is typically more liquid and accessible for short-term operations.

When you add the values of both non-current and current assets, you get the company’s total assets. For example, if non-current assets total 800, and current assets total 1100, the company’s total assets would be 1900.

Liabilities and Their Impact

Liabilities are the financial obligations a company owes to others, and like assets, they are divided into current and non-current liabilities.

Current Liabilities

Current liabilities are obligations that the company needs to pay within one year. These typically include:

  • Short-term loans
  • Trade payables (debts owed to suppliers)
  • Bank overdrafts

Non-Current Liabilities

Non-current liabilities are long-term obligations that extend beyond one year. Common examples are long-term loans or bonds.

Net Assets

Net assets represent the difference between total assets and total liabilities. If a company’s total assets are 1900 and total liabilities are 800, its net assets would be:

  • Net Assets = 1900 - 800 = 1100

This indicates the company’s financial position and shareholder value.

Cash Flow Forecast

A cash flow forecast predicts a company’s future financial position by estimating the inflows and outflows of cash over a set period (often 3 months). This allows companies to assess their ability to cover expenses, investments, and debt.

Importance of Cash Flow Forecast

A cash flow forecast allows the company to:

  • Identify cash shortages or surpluses
  • Plan for the future and ensure liquidity
  • Assess the financial health of operations month-to-month

For example, in one forecast, a company’s cash inflows total 300 while outflows are 306, leading to a negative cash flow of 6 million, showing that adjustments are necessary to avoid financial strain.

Financial Transparency

For publicly traded companies, financial statements must be made public to maintain transparency with investors. This transparency helps investors make informed decisions regarding their investments. Private companies, however, are not obligated to share their financial documents publicly.

Analyzing Financial Statements

There are three main techniques used to analyze financial statements:

  • Ratios
  • Horizontal Analysis
  • Common Size Analysis

Ratios

Financial ratios are an essential tool for analyzing a company’s financial health. They help to assess profitability, liquidity, activity, and leverage.

Profitability Ratios

These ratios measure the ability of a company to generate profits relative to its revenue, assets, and equity. Common profitability ratios include:

  • Gross Profit Margin: Measures profit after production costs.
  • Net Profit Margin: Measures overall profit after all expenses.
  • Return on Assets (ROA) and Return on Equity (ROE): Assess how efficiently the company uses its assets and equity to generate profit.

Liquidity Ratios

Liquidity ratios help determine a company’s ability to meet short-term financial obligations. Key liquidity ratios include:

  • Current Ratio: Measures the ability to cover short-term liabilities with short-term assets.
  • Quick Ratio: A stricter version of the current ratio, excluding inventory from assets.

Activity Ratios

These ratios assess how effectively a company uses its assets to generate revenue. Examples include:

  • Inventory Turnover: Measures how often inventory is sold and replaced.
  • Average Collection Period: Assesses the time taken to collect receivables.

Leverage Ratios

Leverage ratios indicate a company’s reliance on debt for financing. A common leverage ratio is the Debt-to-Asset Ratio, which shows how much of a company’s assets are financed by debt.

Horizontal Analysis

Horizontal analysis compares financial statements over multiple periods to identify trends. This method, also known as trend analysis, helps analyze year-over-year changes. For example, comparing sales revenue from 2018 to 2022 helps identify growth patterns and assess whether the company’s performance is improving or declining over time.

Common Size Analysis

Common size analysis is used to express financial statement items as a percentage of a base figure. This technique allows easier comparisons across periods and companies. In a common size income statement, each item is expressed as a percentage of total sales, while in a common size balance sheet, each item is represented as a percentage of total assets.

Benefits of Common Size Analysis

  • Comparing Companies: Helps compare companies within the same industry.
  • Identifying Financial Strengths and Weaknesses: For example, if a company’s inventory makes up a large portion of its current assets, it may need to address issues with inventory management.

Capital Budgeting

Capital budgeting is the process of evaluating investment opportunities to determine their feasibility and potential return. For example, if you’re considering investing in solar panels for your home, capital budgeting tools can help determine whether the long-term savings justify the initial expense.

Making Investment Decisions

The key goal of capital budgeting is to evaluate whether a proposed investment, like installing solar panels, will generate enough returns over time to justify the upfront cost.

Conclusion

Understanding financial statements and the tools used for their analysis is crucial for assessing a company’s performance and making informed decisions. By using techniques such as ratios, horizontal analysis, and common size analysis, stakeholders can gain valuable insights into a company’s profitability, liquidity, and long-term financial stability.
